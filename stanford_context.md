Automated Product Description Generation for E-commerce via Vision-Language Model Fine-tuningWei Zhao Department of Computer Science Stanford University wzhao18@stanford.edu Xinyan He Department of Computer Science Stanford University xinyanh@stanford.edu Ericka Liu Department of Computer Science Stanford University yilin23@stanford.edu AbstractThe rapid growth of e-commerce has necessitated business owners to provide comprehensive and engaging descriptions for their products to facilitate smooth user shopping experience. However, manually crafting these product descriptions is labor-intensive and time-consuming. Recent advances in deep learning-based generative models offer a promising solution to automate this process. In this paper, we leverage state-of-the-art vision-language models, including CLIP, BLIP, BLIP-2, and OFA, to generate detailed and compelling product descriptions from images and metadata using the Amazon Berkeley Objects dataset. Our evaluation demonstrates significant improvements in the performance of the fine-tuned models over their pretrained versions across numerous metrics, with our best model achieving a two-order-of-magnitude increase in CIDEr score compared to the baseline approach.1. IntroductionIn recent years, the e-commerce sector has experienced substantial growth in scale. To facilitate smooth shopping experience, business owners are required to provide comprehensive information, including both visual displays and textual descriptions, for their products. Writing informative and appealing product descriptions is crucial as they help customers quickly understand product features and differentiate between various options. Traditionally, these product descriptions are crafted manually. Although this method ensures high-quality content, it is labor-intensive, time-consuming, and struggles to keep pace with the expanding variety of products.Recent advances in deep learning-based generative models present a promising solution for generating new content from images and textual prompts. State-of-the-art vision-language models have demonstrated astonishing capabilities across a wide spectrum of multimodal tasks, including image captioning. However, applying existing image captioning models directly to product description generation faces two significant challenges. First, standard image captioning aims to generate sentences that factually describe the elements of a scene in an objective and neutral tone. In contrast, effective product descriptions require an engaging and persuasive tone to captivate potential buyers. Second, while image captioning models focus solely on visual characteristics, it is essential to include product metadata in product descriptions, such as manufacturing details and material composition. This requirement further underscores the inadequacy of existing image captioning models for generating effective product descriptions.In this work, we explore the adaptation of deep learning models for the task of generating product descriptions for e-commerce platforms. Our goal is to build models that take product images and metadata of products as input and output informative and engaging descriptions for them. To accomplish this task, we select four widely used vision-language models as our base models: CLIP, BLIP, BLIP-2, and OFA. These models are fine-tuned using the Amazon Berkeley Objects (ABO) dataset, which includes information on more than 100k products listed on the Amazon platform. We provide a detailed overview of our fine-tuning implementation for each base model in section 4. In addition, we highlight our training strategies to mitigate the substantial resource requirements for large-scale model fine-tuning in section 5, including the comparison between Parameter Freezing and Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA). In section 6, we evaluate the effectiveness of our fine-tuned models using a range of metrics, including BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, and ROUGE-L. Notably, our best-performing model, fine-tuned from the OFA base model, achieves a remarkable two-order-of-magnitude increase in CIDEr score compared to the baseline approach.2. Related WorkVision Pretraining & Language Pretraining. In natural language processing (NLP), learning universal language representations through pretraining has significantly enhanced performance across numerous tasks. Pretrained language models, such as BERT and GPT, leverage vast corpora of text to train large Transformer-based models to learn sophisticated linguistic structures and nuanced sentence semantics in an unsupervised manner. Pretrained models can subsequently be fine-tuned using relatively small, task-specific datasets to render robust performance in a variety of downstream tasks. This pretrain-finetune paradigm not only improves the efficiency of training models for specific tasks but also enhances performance with the transfer of knowledge learned from large-scale datasets.Inspired by NLP's success, numerous efforts have sought to adapt the unsupervised pretraining techniques of transformers to the domain of computer vision. Vision Transformer (ViT) adapts the Transformer architecture for visual data by segmenting an image into patches and treating each as a word token. Pretrained on large image datasets, these models often surpass traditional convolutional neural networks (CNNs) like ResNet in tasks like image recognition and classification.Vision-language Pretraining. This aims to learn joint representations for both vision and language, enabling multi-modal tasks such as image captioning, visual question answering, and text-to-image generation. CLIP employs contrastive learning to predict correct image-caption pairs. Florence adapts contrastively pre-trained models to more downstream tasks. BLIP unifies vision-language understanding and text-generation with a Multimodal mixture of Encoder-Decoder architecture (MED). OFA extends this to a task and modality-agnostic sequence-to-sequence framework. BLIP-2 addresses the soaring cost of pretraining by leveraging frozen pre-trained image encoders and large language models, bridging the modality gap with a lightweight Querying Transformer (Q-Former). This method demonstrated competitive performance while requiring significantly fewer trainable parameters.3. ### 4. Methods
[cite_start]We explore four models: CLIP, BLIP, BLIP-2, and OFA[cite: 69].

#### 4.1. Base Models and Techniques
* [cite_start]**CLIP:** Leverages contrastive learning to jointly pretrain a vision encoder (CNN or ViT) and a GPT-style text encoder[cite: 73, 74, 75]. [cite_start]It predicts correct pairings within an image-text batch[cite: 76, 99].
* [cite_start]**BLIP:** Employs a multimodal mixture of encoder-decoder (MED) scheme consisting of an image-grounded text encoder, an image encoder, and a multimodal decoder[cite: 101].
* [cite_start]**BLIP-2:** Reduces training cost by leveraging frozen pre-trained vision encoders and language models, using a Q-Former as a bridge[cite: 105, 106].
* [cite_start]**OFA:** A transformer-based sequence-to-sequence model that represents various modalities as tokens in a unified vocabulary[cite: 109, 111].
* [cite_start]**LoRA:** A fine-tuning technique that injects trainable low-rank decomposition matrices into specific layers, keeping original weights frozen to reduce computational requirements[cite: 115, 116, 117].

[cite_start]The provided image illustrates how different types of information are used to create a product description for an e-commerce listing, specifically using a rug as an example from the Amazon Berkeley Objects (ABO) dataset[cite: 59, 97].

It breaks down the description generation process into three main components:

* [cite_start]**Product Image:** The visual representation of the item[cite: 78]. [cite_start]The image shows physical details, such as the texture and the fringe on the edges of the rug[cite: 97].
* [cite_start]**Product Metadata:** Structured data (shown here in JSON format) that provides specific facts about the product that might not be obvious from a photo alone, such as the brand ("Rivet"), materials ("Polyester"), and model style ("Moroccan")[cite: 61, 81, 86, 87].
* [cite_start]**Product Description:** The final written text that a customer would read[cite: 89].

#### 4.2. Implementation Details
* [cite_start]**Data Preprocessing:** We filtered for data with images and English descriptions, resulting in 113,203 samples[cite: 121, 122]. [cite_start]JSON metadata was parsed into textual format and streamlined by removing non-essential keywords[cite: 134, 135, 136].
* [cite_start]**CLIP-GPT2:** We integrated CLIP’s image encoder with the GPT2 language model[cite: 138]. [cite_start]Metadata is prepended to the prompt, and visual embeddings are mapped to the language model’s latent space as a learnable prefix[cite: 139, 140]. [cite_start]Both the image encoder and language model were fine-tuned[cite: 143].
* [cite_start]**BLIP Fine-tuning:** Utilized the `BLIPForQuestionAnswering` model, initialized with `blip-vqa-base` weights[cite: 145, 149].
* [cite_start]**BLIP-2 Fine-tuning:** Employed the `blip2-opt-2.7b` variation[cite: 152]. [cite_start]Due to its size (3.1 billion parameters), we compared parameter freezing and LoRA to reduce memory usage[cite: 153, 155].
* [cite_start]**OFA Fine-tuning:** Initialized with `ofa-large` weights[cite: 160]. [cite_start]We used instruction fine-tuning by appending the question "What is the description of the product?"[cite: 164, 165]. [cite_start]To accelerate training, the encoder was frozen and fp16 training was used[cite: 166].


[cite_start]The provided image presents an architectural overview of the **CLIP-GPT2** model, a streamlined system designed to generate product descriptions by combining visual and textual information[cite: 133, 138].

The process works through the following stages:

* [cite_start]**Visual Processing**: A product image is processed through a **CLIP Image Encoder**, which transforms the visual data into an **Image Embedding**[cite: 124, 127, 128].
* [cite_start]**Textual Input**: Optional **Product Metadata** is combined with a specific instruction ("What is the description of the product?") to create a **Prompt Embedding**[cite: 125, 126, 132].
* [cite_start]**Integration**: The image embedding and prompt embedding are joined together to form a single **Input Embedding**[cite: 131].
* [cite_start]**Generation**: This combined embedding is fed into a **GPT2** language model[cite: 130, 138].
* [cite_start]**Output**: The model produces a coherent natural language description, such as "Classic and versatile ballet flat designed for daily wear and superior fit..."[cite: 129].

[cite_start]Unlike some models that only look at one type of data, this architecture allows the language model to use the visual features of the product (like shape and color) alongside factual metadata (like brand or material) to write a more accurate description[cite: 140, 141].

### 5. Training Details
[cite_start]The dataset was split into train, validation, and test sets (7:2:1)[cite: 168]. 

**Hyperparameter Tuning:** For the OFA model, we tested learning rates of 1e-03, 1e-04, and 1e-05; [cite_start]1e-04 was selected for its balance of stability and convergence[cite: 195, 198]. [cite_start]The Adam optimizer was chosen over SGD for producing a lower and more stable loss[cite: 202, 203]. [cite_start]A batch size of 4 was selected to maximize throughput[cite: 207].

[cite_start]**LoRA vs. Parameter Freezing for BLIP-2:** LoRA (rank 256, 2.19% trainable parameters) was compared to parameter freezing (2.86% trainable parameters)[cite: 209, 210]. [cite_start]While both showed similar loss reduction, the estimated training time (approx. 9-10 hours per epoch) exceeded available resources, leading us to discontinue BLIP-2 investigations[cite: 212, 215, 216, 217].
